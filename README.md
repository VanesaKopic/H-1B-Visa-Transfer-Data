#### What Are H-1B Visas  

The H-1B Visa is a temporary non-immigrant visa that allows U.S. employers to hire foreign workers in specialty occupations. Applicants must hold at least a bachelor’s degree, or a foreign equivalent, in a field related to the position they are applying for. Once approved, H-1B visas are granted up to three years and can be extended through renewals (Cahn et al., 2025).

H-1B visas were established under the Immigration Act of 1990 and have undergone several legislative changes. The most prominent and discussed change was the 2004 H-1B Visa Reform Act, which set an annual cap of 65,000 new visas (U.S Citizen and Immigration Services, 2006). Because demand for H-1B visas has exceeded the annual cap every year since 2000, U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) allocates new visas through a lottery system – which was randomly generated by a computer. Petitions for H-1B extensions or changes of employers, however, are cap-exempt and do not reenter the lottery system (Cahn et al., 2025).

Because the H-1B visa is employer-sponsored, workers' legal status in the United States is directly tied to their sponsoring employer. This creates a structural dependency that raises important questions about whether H-1B workers can freely exercise labor market mobility - or whether the terms of their visa effectively bind them to the company that brought them here.
The mobility of H-1B workers therefore has important implications for labor market dynamics and immigration policy, especially as questions about the program's efficacy are being raised with increasing frequency. This project examines how mobility varies across industries and economic conditions to shed light on whether H-1B workers experience uniform constraints or differing opportunities by sector. Extending the analysis beyond technology and situating it within a broader business cycle will provide a more comprehensive perspective on the program.

#### Literature Review 

Existing scholarship on the H-1B program has focused primarily on new petitions and visa extensions, with renewal applications comprising the majority of approvals since 2013\. In peak years, such as 2022, nearly half a million petitions were approved (Cahn et al., 2025). This emphasis in the literature reflects broader policy debates about the role of the H-1B program, particularly addressing the concerns about whether H-1B workers displace native-born employees – which has been a primary talking point since the program's creation (Kirkegaard, 2005; Tigau, 2017\). For many applicants, especially those from India, who face significant employment-based green card backlogs, repeated H-1B extensions function as a bridge to permanent residency rather than simply temporary work authorization (Pierce & Gelatt, 2018). Which is not surprising, as Congress has implemented numerous changes to the program that make it more feasible for H-1B workers to obtain permanent residency through this program (Lowell, 2000).

A related concern in the literature is the degree of employer dependence felt by H-1B workers. This is often because individuals see the H-1B visa program as perpetuating this dependence. H-1B status is employer-sponsored, which begs the question of whether workers face constraints on job mobility and if they are effectively tied to their sponsoring companies (Depew & Norlander, 2013). Inter-firm mobility can be observed through change-of-employer petitions, which, like extensions, are exempt from the annual cap. Although the transfer procedure is administratively feasible, associated costs and uncertainty may still affect workers’ willingness or ability to change employers.

Research examining H-1B mobility generally finds that inter-firm movement does occur and can be substantial. However, the level of mobility appears sensitive to broader economic conditions (Depew et al., 2017; Hu & Lowell, 2019; Kirkegaard, 2005). In periods of strong labor demand, workers operate within a “thick” labor market characterized by abundant job opportunities, making transfers more feasible despite associated costs (Depew et al., 2017). During economic downturns, by contrast, reduced hiring and increased uncertainty can significantly limit mobility.

Much of this research focuses on Indian H-1B workers in the information technology sector, reflecting the industry’s dominant share of H-1B employment (Depew et al., 2013 & 2017; Hu & Lowell, 2019; Pierce & Gelatt, 2018; Mayda et al., 2020; Tigau, 2017). While this focus is empirically justified, it leaves open the question of whether similar mobility patterns exist in other industries that employ H-1B workers but receive comparatively less scholarly attention.

#### Research Question 

This project builds on existing research suggesting that H-1B transfer petitions are sensitive to broader economic conditions. Specifically, it asks whether the relationship between economic conditions and H-1B worker mobility observed in the technological sector is also present in other industries that employ H-1B workers. How does the rate of change-of-employer petitions vary across industries? Focusing on the period from 2021 to 2025, this study examines whether H-1B mobility within major industries shifts across different phases of the U.S. business cycle as the industry sector is known to do.

In brief, the project investigates whether industry-level patterns of H-1B worker mobility, measured through change-of-employer petitions, differ across sectors and whether mobility dynamics identified in the technology sector generalize to the broader H-1B labor market.

#### Data

The primary dataset for this project is drawn from the USCIS H-1B Employer Data Hub, which records employer-level petition outcomes, including change-of-employer approvals. These petitions serve as a measurable indicator of inter-firm mobility among H-1B workers. The dataset includes information on the employer’s industry classification and the fiscal year of the petition, allowing for industry-level analysis. 

To allow for comparison across industries and over time, change-of-employer approvals will be analyzed through the percentage change formula, to account for drastic size of the industry sector compared to the fewer amounts of change-of-employer petitions with other industries. 

While this project attempts to contextualize transfer approval data in relation to broader labor market conditions and national consumer sentiment, the short and turbulent timeframe under analysis makes it challenging to identify a consistent indicator of economic conditions that aligns with transfer approvals. Theoretically, worker mobility is expected to increase when unemployment is low, with H-1B mobility patterns following suit. However, the turbulent post-Covid era complicates analysis in this area.  Comparisons will still be included in the data and analysis to suggest further steps for future projects.

Consumer sentiment and unemployment rates were drawn from a dataset compiled by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis (FRED) from reports gathered by the bank. Since these reports were compiled at various times throughout the year, the analysis averaged the unemployment rates and consumer sentiment to include all reports for one year.

#### Conclusion and Next Steps 

Analysis of non-IT H-1B transfer approvals –  derived by isolating industries outside of 'Professional, Scientific, and Technical Services' from the USCIS Employer Data Hub (2021-2025) –  reveals that non-IT industries trend in the same direction as the IT-adjacent sector, differing only in the rate of change. This suggests that inter-firm mobility among H-1B workers is not an IT-exclusive phenomenon, but rather a broader characteristic of the H-1B program across industries.

This mobility pattern also appears responsive to broader labor market conditions. Consistent with the thick labor market hypothesis outlined in the literature, periods of falling unemployment corresponded with increased transfer approvals, while rising unemployment coincided with declining transfers. Consumer sentiment, however, did not demonstrate a reliable relationship with H-1B transfer rates, suggesting that workers' perceived confidence in the economy does not meaningfully drive inter-firm mobility decisions

This project has several limitations worth acknowledging. The 2021-2025 window is narrow and economically anomalous, encompassing post-COVID labor market distortions, a tech-specific contraction, and aggressive Federal Reserve rate hikes –  all of which may have produced patterns that do not reflect long-run mobility dynamics. Additionally, unemployment is a lagging and broadly measured indicator that may not adequately capture sector-specific labor market conditions relevant to H-1B workers.

Future research should extend the analysis to include pre-COVID figures, which would provide a longer baseline against which current trends can be compared and would allow for a cleaner test of the thick labor market hypothesis across multiple business cycles. As more fiscal year data becomes available, revisiting this analysis to determine whether the patterns observed here persist beyond the post-COVID window would strengthen these findings. Additionally, expanding the dataset to include pre-COVID fiscal years would provide a longer baseline across multiple business cycles. A more concise analysis of a single industry would offer stronger causal insight into what drives H-1B inter-firm mobility beyond the broad economic indicators examined here.
